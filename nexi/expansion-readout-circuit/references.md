# References — Expansion-Readout Circuit

## Primary natural-system literature

- **Roth, G. (2015).** _Convergent evolution of complex brains and high intelligence._ Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B, 370, 20150049. DOI: [10.1098/rstb.2015.0049](https://doi.org/10.1098/rstb.2015.0049).
  _Comparative synthesis of the independent evolution of complex brains and high intelligence across insects, cephalopods, teleost fish, birds, and mammals. Documents the expand-then-read-out cytoarchitectural motif — convergent multimodal afferents on a few large projection neurons diverging onto a vast sheet of tiny, densely packed interneurons in an orthogonal en-passant crossbar, read back out through a sparse projection bottleneck — recurring across anatomically non-homologous substrates. Source of the honeybee mushroom-body (~300,000 Kenyon cells, 800 projection neurons, ~1M presynaptic contacts) and octopus vertical-lobe (~1.8M afferents × ~26M interneurons × 65,000 projection neurons) quantitative circuit data, and of the soma-miniaturisation / packing-density observation (Kenyon somata ~15× denser than the densest vertebrate neurons)._

## Supporting circuit-level and comparative literature

- **Shomrat, T., Zarrella, I., Fiorito, G., & Hochner, B. (2008).** _The octopus vertical lobe modulates short-term learning rate and uses LTP to store long-term memory._ Current Biology, 18, 337–342. DOI: [10.1016/j.cub.2008.01.056](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cub.2008.01.056).
  _Mechanistic evidence for the vertical-lobe read-out stage: the lobe modulates short-term learning rate and hosts activity-dependent long-term potentiation. Grounds the "sparse readout as substrate of selection / associative recall" claim in a non-vertebrate expansion-readout circuit._

- **Farris, S. M., & Schulmeister, S. (2011).** _Parasitoidism, not sociality, is associated with the evolution of elaborate mushroom bodies in the brains of hymenopteran insects._ Proceedings of the Royal Society B, 278, 940–951. DOI: [10.1098/rspb.2010.2161](https://doi.org/10.1098/rspb.2010.2161).
  _Identifies the ecological driver of mushroom-body elaboration as spatial-navigation demand (host-finding in parasitoids), not sociality. Relevant to when an expansion-readout stage pays: the natural expansion layer scales with associative / navigational demand rather than with a single social variable._

- **Jarvis, E. D., et al. (2005).** _Avian brains and a new understanding of vertebrate brain evolution._ Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 6, 151–159. DOI: [10.1038/nrn1606](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn1606).
  _Revised homology framework for the avian pallium. Supports treating corvid / psittacid pallial circuits as a genuinely independent instance of the motif rather than a mammalian homologue — load-bearing for the non-homologous-substrates (convergence) claim._
