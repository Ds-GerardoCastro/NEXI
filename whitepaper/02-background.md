# 2. Background and Related Work

This section surveys the four literatures the white paper sits between: world models in AI, scaling-law debates, comparative cognition relevant to AI design, and pattern-catalog approaches in AI and software engineering. The survey is _purposive_ — only the parts of each literature that bear on the program's direction are covered, and the survey does not aspire to comprehensive coverage of any of the four. The position of this work in relation to the four is stated at the end of the section.

## 2.1 World models in AI

The notion of an internal world model — a learned representation that allows a system to anticipate environmental dynamics, predict the consequences of its own actions, and plan over a horizon — is older than modern deep learning. Schmidhuber (1990) proposed recurrent networks as world-model components in the context of reinforcement learning, with the controller and the world model jointly trained to maximise predictive accuracy [Schmidhuber 1990]. Ha and Schmidhuber (2018) reintroduced the framework into the deep-learning era with a system that learned a compressed latent dynamics model and trained a controller in the model's "imagination" before deploying it to the actual environment [Ha & Schmidhuber 2018].

The recent inflection in world-model research is the Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture family. JEPA, V-JEPA, and I-JEPA [Assran et al. 2023; Bardes et al. 2024] depart from raw-pixel prediction in favour of predicting future _embeddings_ — representations of how the world will look at a higher level of abstraction than pixel intensities. The architectural commitment is that learning to predict the right embedding implicitly forces the encoder to capture causal dynamics, while sidestepping the wasted capacity that pixel-level prediction allocates to texture noise. The JEPA family is the most prominent technical instantiation of the world-model thesis as articulated by LeCun [LeCun 2022], and the research direction is the central engineering response to the architectural critique developed in §3.1.

The white paper accepts the world-model framing without modification. Its contribution is orthogonal to JEPA-style work: where the world-model framing answers _what is missing_, the program in this paper answers _what kind of internal architecture_ should be built once the framing is accepted. The two are complementary rather than competing — a V-JEPA-style world model that adopts NEXI collections is the explicit integration target the program looks toward (§7.3).

Adjacent literatures bear on the world-model framing without being its direct lineage. Predictive coding and the broader free-energy formulation in computational neuroscience are conceptually aligned with prediction-as-architecture but operate at a level of mathematical abstraction that has not yet produced deployable deep-learning artefacts comparable to the JEPA family. Self-supervised representation learning more broadly is the substrate within which the world-model thesis is now being prosecuted; the distinction the white paper draws is that joint-embedding _predictive_ approaches are architecturally aligned with the world-model thesis in a way that joint-embedding _contrastive_ approaches (CLIP-family) are not — contrastive learning produces aligned representations, not predictive ones, and the architectural critique requires prediction.

## 2.2 Scaling-law debates

Empirical scaling laws of the LLM era are now well-documented. Kaplan et al. (2020) established power-law relationships between model loss and parameter count, dataset size, and training compute [Kaplan et al. 2020]. Hoffmann et al. (2022) refined the relationships in the Chinchilla paper, deriving compute-optimal allocations of parameters to data and challenging the prior community default of parameter-heavy training [Hoffmann et al. 2022]. The empirical record across 2020–2026 has consistently shown emergent capabilities at scale that smaller models lack — a pattern Sutton (2019) crystallised as the "Bitter Lesson:" general methods leveraging compute have repeatedly defeated AI approaches that rely on human-derived structure [Sutton 2019].

The architectural-critique tradition has not denied the empirical record. LeCun (2022, 2023, 2024) and Marcus (2022) have argued that the scaling pattern has limits, but that the limit is not in any single capability scaling has not yet reached — it is in the _kind_ of failure modes that persist regardless of scale: hallucination of causal structure, brittleness in physical environments, planning failures at horizons exceeding training-context patterns [LeCun 2022, 2024; Marcus 2022]. The architectural critique's prediction is that scaling token-prediction will produce more fluent token-prediction, not the world models that planning requires. The 2020–2026 empirical record is consistent with this prediction at the failure-mode level even where scaling has been dramatic at the capability level.

The white paper takes the architectural-critique side of the debate but qualifies the position: the Bitter Lesson is correctly applied _within_ a substrate, and the template critique (§3.2) argues the perceptron substrate is one biased sample of the intelligence-design space. Sutton's lesson and the program in this paper are not in conflict; the lesson predicts that within each substrate, general methods plus compute will outperform structure-imposing alternatives — exactly what per-pattern falsifiability (§4.6) is designed to test.

A related strand of the scaling debate concerns the mathematical-evolutionary structure of capacity itself. Turner et al. (2026) [the source paper for the Bounded Cognitive Architecture collection, §6.2] derive a model in which capacity contributes linearly to recall efficacy while regulation contributes sublinearly. The result is congruent with Chinchilla's data-vs-parameters reframing — read as "data is a capacity expansion" — and provides an evolutionary-optimality argument for the empirical scaling laws rather than only a fitting-curve argument. The white paper takes this congruence as evidence that the scaling debate and the comparative-cognition debate are not separate conversations; they are the same conversation with different vocabularies.

## 2.3 Comparative cognition relevant to AI design

The catalog draws on four sub-literatures of comparative cognition, each used for a specific architectural purpose.

**Animal-communication ecology and social-network cognition.** Work on communication networks in birds [McGregor & Dabelsteen 1996; Snijders & Naguib 2017; Hagedoorn et al. 2026] motivates the Distributed Social Cognition collection (§6.1). The literature documents that information about agents flows through observation of third-party interactions, identity from acoustic signatures, multi-channel grounding, and structured spatial aggregation — patterns the perceptron lineage has rarely engaged with as a system. The earliest formal framings include the "information centres" hypothesis [Ward & Zahavi 1973] and colony-acoustics research [Waas et al. 2005]; the more recent network-analytic work makes the patterns mathematically specifiable in a form the catalog can adopt.

**Comparative cognitive capacity research.** The mathematical-evolutionary work on cognitive capacity and control [Turner et al. 2026] motivates the Bounded Cognitive Architecture collection (§6.2). The relevant claim is that cognition is allocation under metabolic cost, that the deployment niche shapes the optimum, and that storage capacity is prerequisite to regulation. The phylogenetic placements span jellyfish, sea anemones, nematodes, flatworms, tardigrades, corvids, elephants, cetaceans, and great apes — a span that the perceptron-lineage design space cannot reach without the catalog's ingestion discipline. Adjacent capacity-and-control literature in cognitive science (working-memory research, retro-cue task data) is referenced through this source.

**Non-neural intelligence.** Literature on bacterial decision-making [Nesin & Chandrankunnel 2025], slime mold optimisation [Nakagaki et al. 2000], and plant signalling [Trewavas 2014] documents intelligence implementations that operate without nervous systems. This sub-literature motivates the Acerebrate Decision-Making collection (§6.3) and is the sharpest empirical pressure the program puts on the perceptron template's centralised-controller assumption. The methodological challenge for AI engineering is that the substrate is so different from current AI architectures that transfer requires explicit substrate-independence claims rather than mechanical translation — exactly the form §6.3's composition shape takes.

**Cephalopod cognition and embodied invertebrate intelligence.** Hochner (2012) documents distributed peripheral cognition in octopuses; Webb (2002) documents complex adaptive behaviour from insect-navigation closed sensorimotor loops without a central planner. Buzsáki (2006) provides phase-coded-dynamics evidence across many invertebrate brains. These sub-literatures are not yet anchoring collections but are on the catalog's Watch List (§4.8) as priority sources for forthcoming ingestion. A further literature on collective and distributed cognition more broadly [Hutchins 1995; Seeley 2010] sits alongside and informs the constellation-collection shape.

The survey is purposive. It does not duplicate any of the four sub-literatures' breadth; it identifies the parts of each that are most fertile as design sources for AI architectures. A more comprehensive comparative-cognition survey would belong in a different paper.

## 2.4 Pattern catalogs in AI and software engineering

The pattern-catalog tradition originates with Alexander's _A Pattern Language_ (1977), which catalogued architectural patterns in built environments alongside their forces, contexts, and resolutions [Alexander 1977]. The Gang-of-Four book [Gamma et al. 1994] adapted the pattern-language form to object-oriented software engineering, codifying twenty-three named design patterns in a format that included intent, motivation, structure, applicability, and consequences. The pattern-catalog form has since proliferated across software engineering — enterprise application patterns, microservice patterns, data engineering patterns, security patterns — without a single canonical reference superseding the originals.

Pattern catalogs in AI specifically are sparser. Practitioner-facing pattern collections exist for prompt engineering, agent-architecture topics, and machine-learning system design, but they vary in citation discipline and falsifiability commitment. The closest formal work is in software-engineering reviews of ML system patterns (deployment patterns, MLOps patterns, retrieval-augmented generation patterns) — these are pragmatically useful but not organised by biological provenance and not committed to per-pattern falsifiability in the form §4.6 specifies.

To the author's knowledge, no prior pattern catalog organises AI architectural primitives by biological provenance with the discipline of (a) peer-reviewed source citation, (b) per-pattern falsifiable hypothesis, (c) collection-level system hypothesis, (d) status-as-audit-trail, and (e) deterministic citation verification. NEXI's contribution within the pattern-catalog tradition is the disciplined synthesis of these five commitments around a specific source-selection bias.

## 2.5 Position of this work

The Nature Intelligence Project sits at the intersection of the four literatures surveyed above. It is congruent with world-model research at the architectural level (§2.1) and adopts that direction without modification. It takes a specific qualified position in the scaling-law debate (§2.2): the Bitter Lesson applies within a substrate, and the program's contribution is to widen the substrate — a position the Turner et al. capacity-and-control framing supports rather than contradicts. It draws on comparative cognition purposively (§2.3) for engineering-ready primitives rather than as a survey. It inherits the pattern-catalog form from Alexander and the Gang-of-Four (§2.4) but commits to peer-reviewable provenance and per-pattern falsifiability that those traditions did not require.

The contribution is the _methodology_ connecting comparative cognition to AI design with provenance and falsifiability preserved end-to-end, instantiated in a catalog that has produced three structurally distinct collection shapes from three peer-reviewed sources. The remainder of the paper develops that methodology (§4), the framework (§5), and the demonstration cases (§6), then discusses limitations and counter-arguments (§7), maps future work (§8), and concludes (§9).
